Adopt

If you're looking to adopt a Norwegian Elkhound, just know they can be hard to find. You may have to be very patient or be willing to drive a long distance to get your Elkhound. If you are really set on adopting, here are some tips to help you get an Elkhound.

Find a Shelter/Rescue Group

Trying to find all the shelters and rescue groups in your area can be time consuming. Luckily, there are two good sites that search nearby shelters/rescue groups for you, PetFinder and Adopt a Pet. I personally prefer Petfinder as you can better customize your search but both sites are good to explore. One site may show results the other does not.

Both sites also offer searches to find individual shelters and rescue groups.

Questions to Ask

Just like with breeders, you should always ask questions to find out more about your future pet.

  • What is the dog's personality like?
  • How old is the dog?
  • What is the dog's energy level?
  • Is the dog house-trained?
  • Does the dog get along with other animals?
  • How does the dog act around various people such as the staff/foster parents, visitors, and children?
  • Has the dog ever snapped at anyone or shown any aggressive behavior to their knowledge?
  • Does the dog have any known health issues?

Shelter vs Rescue Group

You may be wondering if it's better to get a dog from a shelter or a rescue group. It's a different experience for everyone, but here's what you can commonly expect at each place.

Overall, your dog/puppy will be up-to-date on shots and neutered/spayed no matter if you get it from a shelter or rescue group.

Shelters

At a shelter, you will go through an interview process once you have picked out your dog/puppy. If the interviewer deems you fit for the dog/puppy, you will get to take your dog/puppy home that day.

Rescue Groups

If you go through a rescue group, you will have to fill out an online application for the dog/puppy you want. Next, your application will be read over by the staff and you will be notified if they find your application acceptable. Next, you will set up a meeting with the foster parent to meet the dog/puppy. If you are set on that dog/puppy, they will set up a home interview. If the home interview goes well, you will then get your dog/puppy.

While the process sounds tedious, the advantage to getting a dog/puppy from a rescue is that the foster parent can provide you with more information about the dog/puppy than a shelter can.
